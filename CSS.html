Erlkönig Analysis

Alexander Burdiss

In Erlkönig, Schubert utilized the Lied tradition to its fullest. Schubert enhanced the meaning of Goethe’s poem and added more to the story than was capable through words alone. Through ingeniously crafted melodies and textures, Schubert is able to set each phrase to music perfectly, in an ever changing way. Schubert’s Erlkönig beautifully depicts the setting, characters, and their emotions through his use of melodic, harmonic, and rhythmic devices.

Goethe’s poem Erlkönig contains three characters and a narrator. The narrator serves to set the scene at the beginning and end. In the poem, a young boy and his father ride on horseback through the forest as an Elf King tries to lure the young boy away from his father. The father doesn’t seem to notice the Elf King until it is too late. Although Goethe set the poem in strophic form, Schubert sets the poem musically in a through-composed form. By using the through-composed style Schubert is able to change the musical style (harmonies, texture, tessitura, etc.) depending on the character that is speaking. Schubert is also able to show character development and story arc through his use of through-composed form. Strophic form would not be able to capture this effect, because the characters switch off in the text at irregular intervals.

The piano part of Schubert’s Erlkönig does not function strictly as an accompanimental role; rather, it serves the role of another character and helps to paint the scene that the characters interact with. In mm. 1-14, the repeated triplet figure played in right hand depicts the canter of the horse, and the chord changes set an eerie mood, akin to the dark, misty, and windy forest in which this scene takes place. The rising and falling melodic pattern played by the left hand in m. 2 depicts the wind blowing through the forest. This ‘wind’ is most present in the sections where the narrator is speaking, but is mostly forgotten about in the central action of the poem where the characters exist. The piano plays the independent role of the horse and the landscape in this song, and paints clearly in the listener’s mind the setting of the scene.

Different characters are expressed through the use of different tessituras and styles of singing. The son character sings in the high tessitura, ranging from F4 to Gb5, but favors the higher tessitura of his range. When the boy is singing, as in mm. 73-79, the piano also favors the higher tessitura, playing the repeated triplet pattern from the opening, modulated up a fifth from G to D. The son character’s frightened mood is also expressed effectively through the music. For example, in m. 58, the Elf king sings in a more relaxed style than even the father, trying to entice the son away. In m. 72, the end of the Elf King’s phrase is marked at a pianississimo dynamic, and the piano abruptly breaks into the repeated triplet theme from the opening on an offbeat and at a subito forte dynamic, signaling the entrance of the son character. This startling entrance depicts how frightened the son character is in this moment.

The father character sings in the lower tessitura, ranging from C4 to D5, and favors the lower tessitura of his range. Schubert highlights the natural difference in tessitura between young and adult male voices in his characters. The piano also highlights this difference in tessitura, following the higher and lower ranges each character sings in. The clearest example of this difference happens between mm. 97 and 111. In m. 105, the measure between the switch in character from son to father, the chords in the right hand of the piano descend, and the repeated triplet pattern is modulated down a third to match the lower tessitura of the father.

When the Elf King sings, his range spans the complete tessitura of the piece, ranging from E4 to G5, not favoring the high or low of his range. The Elf King sings in a melismatic style, whereas the Father, Son, and Narrator sing in a syllabic style. The piano texture is only different when the Elf King sings; the first entrance of the elf king is accompanied by a broken triplet pattern. The second time the Elf King sings, the piano plays a broken chord accompaniment with a drum bass in the left hand. The final time the Elf King sings, he sings in the aggravated style of both the father and the son. The Elf King has grown frustrated at this point, and has entered the vicinity of the son and father symbolically and literally. The lighter style present in the earlier entries of the Elf King highlight his evil motives, because he is trying to lure the child away from his father by using sweeter, more songlike melodies.

The harmonies of this work also help to showcase each character. When the father sings, the mode is generally minor, with a few major keyed exceptions. When the son enters, the harmony is exclusively diminished. This helps to show how the son is scared, and feeling unstable. Every time the Elf King sings, the mode modulates to major. This modulation furthers the Elf King’s motives, because he is trying to lure the child away by using sweet harmonies and songlike melodies.

In the first two entries, the Elf King makes futile attempts to persuade the son to come with him. The first attempt has a moderate character with gentle melody, and is in the key of the Relative Major. The second attempt has a songlike character with an even sweeter melody, and is in the key of C major. The Elf King gets frustrated after singing his second phrase, and is more determined by the third phrase to capture the son. The third entry of the Elf King starts on a Neapolitan Chord. This chord is presented almost as another modulation to major, but is in the same minor key the son and father sang in prior to his entrance. The piano texture does not change for this entrance, and is the same texture used during both the son and fathers sections throughout the piece. By having the Elf King enter the third time in the same key as the son and father, and with the same texture, it is as if the Elf King has entered the same realm as the son and father, and is able to finally capture the son by doing so. Through these subtle changes in texture, mode, and mood, Schubert creates a heightened sense of drama that moves the plot forward and enhances the meaning of the text in a profound way, creating a work of music that can move the listener emotionally and intellectually.

The song ends with a line sung without accompaniment. This setting of the text “In his arms the child lay dead” stands out from the rest of the song because of its lack of accompaniment, and captures the very depressed and lonely emotion present in the text. The words “lay dead” are also set apart from the rest of the phrase by a piano chord. By suspending the phrase “lay dead” schubert is able to create one last bit of suspense by making the listener wait for a conclusion to even the final phrase. The final cadence played by the piano is concise and appropriate. It helps to underline the crudity and gravity of the situation, rather than trying to extend the feeling of grief. The abrupt ending is much more fitting to the child’s abrupt death.

Schubert manages to bring the characters in Erlkönig to life in a way that enhances the meaning of Goethe’s original poem. By using range, texture, and by treating the piano as an independent voice, Schubert is able to paint a musical soundscape that immerses the listener in a windy, terror filled landscape, and effectively and profoundly tells the narrative of the Elf King. Schubert took the words of Goethe, and created something much more artistic and expressive. In the words of Heinrich Heine, “Where words leave off, music begins”.
